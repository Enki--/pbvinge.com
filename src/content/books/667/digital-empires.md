# Digital Empires

_The Global Battle to Regulate Technology_

## üéôÔ∏è Comps Prep (Oral Comprehensive Exam)

- If the US, China, and the EU can export ‚Äútech companies, technologies, and rules‚Äù beyond their borders, then global competition increasingly runs through ‚Äúdigital empires,‚Äù because influence comes from governing foreign populations‚Äô data/information without formal conquest. So what for strategy: treat tech governance as geopolitical terrain, not ‚Äúdomestic policy.‚Äù (PDF p. 13)
    
- When a state chooses a market-driven, state-driven, or rights-driven regulatory model, then it gets predictable tradeoffs in innovation, control, and rights‚Äîand exports those tradeoffs abroad‚Äîbecause each model reflects distinct ideological commitments embedded in law and institutions. - Bridge: This aligns with Patterson‚Äôs strategic-competition framing of IW-by-other-means by showing regulation/standards/data rules as non-kinetic levers that can decide ‚Äúthe battle for the soul of the digital economy.‚Äù (PDF p. 33)

## Course Lens

- How does this text define/illuminate **irregular warfare**?
    
    - Not an IW text per se, but it reframes ‚Äúcompetition‚Äù as governance over populations via control of data/information and the digital rules that structure daily life‚Äîan influence contest that operates persistently ‚Äúbelow war.‚Äù (PDF p. 13)
        
    - It highlights how states (and firms) can shape foreign societies without formal political control‚Äîan ‚Äúinformal empire‚Äù logic relevant to modern influence campaigns and political warfare. (PDF p. 13)
        
- What does it imply about **power/control**, **success metrics**, and **timeline** in IW?
    
    - Power is exercised through rule-setting and system design: **private power** (dominant firms), **infrastructure power** (networks/standards), and **regulatory power** (laws with extraterritorial effects). (PDF p. 29)
        
    - Control is both **horizontal** (empire vs empire) and **vertical** (state vs platform); success is durable compliance, dependency, and norm adoption‚Äînot battlefield outcomes. (PDF p. 33)
        
    - Timeline is long: infrastructure and regulatory diffusion create path dependencies that are hard to unwind once embedded in foreign societies. (PDF p. 13, PDF p. 29)
        
- How does it connect to **strategic competition**?
    
    - Regulation functions as geopolitical strategy by externalizing a model‚Äôs values (freedom/market, control/state, rights/law) into other jurisdictions and shaping global standards‚Äîdeciding whose rules govern the digital order. (PDF p. 13, PDF p. 30‚Äì33)

## ‚úÖ Direct Responses to Seminar Questions

- **Q:** How do the three digital empires reshape global competition?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - They reframe competition as a struggle over **governance of the digital economy**‚Äîwho sets the rules that shape foreign societies‚Äîrather than only territorial or military contests. (PDF p. 13)
            
        - Each empire projects influence by exporting a domestic model abroad, pulling other states ‚Äúinto the orbits‚Äù of competing regulatory regimes. (PDF p. 13)
            
        - Competition becomes multi-channel: **private power** (US firms), **infrastructure power** (China‚Äôs network buildout), and **regulatory power** (EU rule-setting) operate as distinct vectors of influence. (PDF p. 29)
            
        - Rivalry is not only interstate (‚Äúhorizontal‚Äù battles) but also state‚Äìplatform (‚Äúvertical‚Äù battles) that determine whether governments or firms govern key social spaces. (PDF p. 33)
            
        - Unaligned markets become ‚Äúbattlefields,‚Äù where local governments weigh American platforms, Chinese infrastructure, and European law when choosing digital futures. (PDF p. 29)
            
- **Q:** How does regulation operate as geopolitical strategy?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - Regulation exports values and constraints: by pushing a model abroad, states seek to ‚Äúshift the rest of the world closer to the norms and values inherent in their ‚Ä¶ models.‚Äù (PDF p. 26)
            
        - The EU‚Äôs strategy relies heavily on **regulatory power** that ‚Äúentrenches European digital norms across the global marketplace,‚Äù affecting non-EU users through corporate compliance. (PDF p. 29)
            
        - China‚Äôs strategy relies heavily on **infrastructure power**, building ‚Äúcritical digital network infrastructures‚Äù abroad via firms with close state ties, embedding standards and dependencies. (PDF p. 29)
            
        - The US strategy historically relied heavily on **private power**‚Äîdominant companies shaping the global digital sphere through products/services and business practices. (PDF p. 29)
            
        - Regulation also becomes coercive statecraft in the tech war context‚Äîe.g., export controls and entity listings used to constrain adversary capabilities and shape the technological frontier. (PDF p. 217‚Äì218)
            
- **Q:** What implications arise from divergent standards?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - Divergent standards intensify ‚Äúbattle lines‚Äù where models collide‚Äîespecially around speech/content, privacy/data flows, and security‚Äîmaking interoperability and shared governance harder. (PDF p. 33‚Äì35)
            
        - Firms face conflicting compliance demands, pushing them toward market exits, localization, or selective compliance‚Äîfueling fragmentation dynamics. (PDF p. 33, PDF p. 206)
            
        - Divergence heightens alliance management problems: democracies must reconcile different balances of rights, markets, and security to avoid being out-competed by the coherence of authoritarian control models. (PDF p. 30‚Äì33)
            
        - Divergent regimes amplify national security anxieties: data access, espionage risk, and supply-chain leverage become central, not peripheral, to tech policy. (PDF p. 28‚Äì29)
            
        - The information environment becomes a strategic battleground: standards shape what populations see, share, and trust‚Äîraising stakes for legitimacy and democratic resilience. (PDF p. 33, PDF p. 449)
            
- **Q:** Which model is best positioned for future competition?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - Bradford argues the conventional US‚ÄìChina binary is ‚Äúflawed,‚Äù because the EU constitutes a third center of regulatory gravity shaping the future digital order. (PDF p. 30)
            
        - She argues the ‚ÄúAmerican market-driven regulatory model is fading,‚Äù leaving many countries to coalesce behind either a Chinese state-driven variant or core tenets of the European rights-driven model. (PDF p. 30)
            
        - The EU is described as ‚Äúa more desirable alternative model in the democratic world,‚Äù while China‚Äôs model is ‚Äúappealing to authoritarian leaders‚Äù and some developing countries. (PDF p. 31)
            
        - Practical ‚Äúpositioning‚Äù therefore looks bifurcated: the EU can lead in rule-setting diffusion; China can expand via infrastructure/standards and offer an authoritarian toolkit; the US retains private power but faces declining model legitimacy absent regulatory adaptation. (PDF p. 29‚Äì31)
            
- **Q:** Does the US reliance on private-sector innovation strengthen or weaken national security when compared to the EU and Chinese regulatory models?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - It strengthens national power through the global dominance of US tech firms (private power), but creates governance gaps when market incentives conflict with societal resilience, privacy, and democratic integrity. (PDF p. 29, PDF p. 26)
            
        - The US model‚Äôs ‚Äúlimited role for the government‚Äù is explicitly bounded by national security/cybersecurity where the state ‚Äúcan and must work alongside private companies‚Äù‚Äîsuggesting security dependence on private capability. (PDF p. 33)
            
        - China‚Äôs state-driven model can align firms with regime objectives and security priorities (and control information flows), but at the cost of rights and openness‚Äîturning technology into a tool to ‚Äúempower the state, not its people.‚Äù (PDF p. 33)
            
        - The EU‚Äôs rights-driven model prioritizes protections that can bolster democratic resilience and trust (privacy, constraints on harmful content and market power), but can be portrayed as harming innovation and free speech‚Äîraising tradeoff questions in competition. (PDF p. 28‚Äì29)
            
        - Bradford‚Äôs framing implies national security is not just ‚Äúcapability,‚Äù but also legitimacy and governance: democracies can lose if they lose either to China (horizontal) or to tech giants (vertical). (PDF p. 33)
            
- **Q:** What does Bradford‚Äôs work reveal about the strategic significance of data?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - Data is the central resource of digital power: ‚Äúpersonal data is the most valuable resource in the 21st-century digital economy‚Äù and fuels tech firms‚Äô power‚Äîmaking data governance core to national influence. (PDF p. 11)
            
        - Data flows become a geopolitical fault line: EU privacy constraints and US surveillance practices can disrupt transatlantic commerce and force firms to reconsider market presence. (PDF p. 29, PDF p. 261‚Äì263)
            
        - Data access and control underpin both vertical battles (states vs firms) and horizontal rivalry (empire vs empire), linking domestic legal regimes to global alignment choices. (PDF p. 33)
            
        - In authoritarian contexts, data enables surveillance, censorship, and behavioral manipulation, making exported infrastructure and standards strategically consequential (control at scale). (PDF p. 102‚Äì103, PDF p. 38)
