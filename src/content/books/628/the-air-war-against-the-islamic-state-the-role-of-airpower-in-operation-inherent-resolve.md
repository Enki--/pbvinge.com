# The Air War Against the Islamic State

## Online Description

Airpower played a pivotal role in the U.S.-led fight against the Islamic State from 2014 to 2019 and contributed to the success of Operation Inherent Resolve, but airpower alone would not have been likely to defeat the militant organization.
  
## **üî´ Author Background**

- **Becca Wasser** is a defense analyst who has worked at RAND Project AIR FORCE and later at CNAS, focusing on U.S. defense strategy, wargaming, and force posture in the Middle East and Indo-Pacific.
    
- **Stacie L. Pettyjohn** is a former RAND senior political scientist and director in Project AIR FORCE whose work centers on airpower, basing, and operational concepts; she has written extensively on coercion and air campaigns.
    
- **Karl P. Mueller** is a longtime RAND political scientist and airpower theorist specializing in coercive air campaigns, including editing _Precision and Purpose: Airpower in the Libyan Civil War_.
    
- **Jeffrey Martini** is a RAND Middle East specialist whose research covers regional politics, security forces, and civil conflict, grounding the report‚Äôs treatment of Iraqi and Syrian partners.
    
- The broader author team blends quantitative analysts, Middle East regional experts, and airpower practitioners, reflecting RAND‚Äôs role as a policy-relevant, empirically grounded think tank closely linked to USAF and OSD.

## **üîç Author‚Äôs Main Issue / Thesis**

- The authors ask how airpower was actually used in Operation Inherent Resolve (OIR), how effective it was in achieving the goal of ISIS‚Äôs ‚Äúenduring defeat,‚Äù and what limits shaped that effectiveness. They respond directly to claims that the air war was ‚Äúunserious‚Äù or overly constrained and that a more aggressive campaign would have quickly destroyed ISIS. (p. 6, p. 12)
    
- Using new data sets on strikes, targets, and sorties, they argue that **airpower was indispensable to defeating ISIS but not sufficient on its own**; local ground partners and a political strategy were central to the coalition‚Äôs theory of victory. (p. 10, p. 298)
    
- They show that the **close fight** (CAS and immediate counterland) had far more direct impact on ISIS‚Äôs center of gravity‚Äîterritorial control‚Äîthan the **deep fight** (strategic attack and interdiction), although deep strikes degraded ISIS finances and constrained maneuver. (p. 299)
    
- They conclude that while less restrictive rules or faster escalation might have modestly accelerated ISIS‚Äôs defeat, political, partner-capacity, and urban terrain constraints meant airpower could not deliver a Desert Storm‚Äìstyle quick victory against a dug-in proto-state. (p. 298‚Äì299)
    
- The book ultimately frames OIR as an archetype of **limited-liability, partner-centric air campaigns** likely to characterize future U.S. wars.

## **üß≠ One-Paragraph Overview**

  

This RAND study provides a strategic and operational history of the air war against ISIS from 2014‚Äì2019, organized around three campaign phases (degrade, counterattack, and defeat) and three types of air activity: the close fight, the deep fight, and enabling functions like DCA and mobility. Chapters 2‚Äì3 narrate how U.S. and coalition airpower supported a ‚Äúby, with, and through‚Äù strategy to roll back ISIS‚Äôs caliphate, tracing changing sortie levels, targets, and partner operations. Chapters 4‚Äì6 dive into case studies of six major battles (Mosul Dam, Kobani, Tikrit, Ramadi, Mosul, Raqqa), four deliberate deep operations (Tidal Wave II, Point Blank, logistics and GLOC interdiction), and enabling missions (DCA against Russia/Assad/Iranian aircraft, and air mobility, especially tankers). Using large strike and sortie data sets plus ~50 interviews, the authors assess what airpower actually did, how limits (political, legal, partner-dependent, and logistical) shaped its employment, and where debates about ‚Äúunderutilized‚Äù airpower are supported‚Äîor contradicted‚Äîby the evidence. The conclusion distills lessons and recommendations for future air wars against both nonstate and near-peer adversaries, emphasizing the need to integrate close and deep fights, strengthen C2 and data systems, and plan for long-duration, coalition, partner-led campaigns.

- **Limits on airpower** ‚Äì Political risk tolerance, legal/ROE constraints, partner capacity, and ISR/PGM/tanker bottlenecks all shaped tempo, target sets, and campaign design.

- **Adaptation & learning** ‚Äì Strike cells, devolved targeting authority, deep-strike design (Tidal Wave II/Point Blank), and DCA tactics evolved over time.
    
- **Efficacy** ‚Äì Tactical success (targets destroyed) did not always translate into rapid operational or political gains; territory became the central measure of success. (p. 299)
    
- **Alliance/coalition dynamics** ‚Äì Coalition politics and basing access constrained some options but also broadened legitimacy and resources.
    
- **Domain interplay** ‚Äì Air/land integration was decisive; DCA, space-enabled ISR, and information flows underpinned both close and deep fights.

## **üîë Top Takeaways**

- **Airpower was central but not sovereign.** OIR confirms that even very capable air forces cannot substitute for competent ground forces in destroying a proto-state; airpower‚Äôs greatest effects came when tightly integrated with Iraqi, Kurdish, and SDF ground offensives. (p. 298‚Äì299)
    
- **The close fight mattered more than the deep fight for ISIS‚Äôs center of gravity.** Deep strikes badly damaged ISIS‚Äôs finances and some operational systems, but territorial control‚ÄîISIS‚Äôs core source of power and legitimacy‚Äîwas broken primarily through CAS and immediate counterland in support of local ground forces. (p. 299)
    
- **A limited-liability, partner-centric strategy was the real constraint.** The ‚Äúslow‚Äù pace of OIR reflected deliberate political choices to minimize U.S. casualties and avoid large ground deployments, not simply underusing airpower. (p. 6, p. 299)
    
- **Urban warfare blurs CAS and strategic attack.** Mosul and Raqqa show how precision airpower in dense cities becomes an attritional, street-by-street endeavor, consuming vast amounts of ISR, PGMs, and tankers while raising acute civilian-harm dilemmas.
    
- **C2 and authority design are critical.** Strike cells, devolved targeting engagement authority (TEA), and CJTF‚ÄìCFACC coordination evolved over time, improving responsiveness but also highlighting persistent seams between land-centric and air-centric headquarters. (p. 25‚Äì26, p. 133‚Äì134)
    
- **Enablers are often the tightest bottleneck.** Tankers (‚ÄúNo Kick Ass Without Tanker Gas‚Äù), ISR platforms, and airlift were more binding constraints than bombers or fighters, shaping sortie patterns and campaign tempo. (p. 293)
    
- **OIR extends post-1991 trends.** As in Kosovo, Afghanistan, and Libya, airpower enabled local partners and managed escalation but did not deliver a decisive quick victory; it functioned as an instrument of limited war, bounded by domestic politics and coalition dynamics.
    
- **Data for assessing airpower remain messy.** Even with tens of thousands of strike and sortie records, the authors stress that ‚Äúcriteria for military effectiveness are fuzzy and subject to interpretation,‚Äù a caution against over-confident claims about what airpower ‚Äúcould have‚Äù done. (p. 8)

### **Chapter One: Airpower and the War Against the Islamic State**

  

**Summary:**

Chapter One introduces the political and strategic context of the fight against ISIS and situates OIR within broader debates about airpower‚Äôs potential and limits. It highlights competing narratives: critics who saw the air war as ‚Äúunserious‚Äù and overly constrained, and others who argued that constraints and a partner-centric approach were appropriate to U.S. interests. (p. 6, p. 12) The authors explain that their goal is not a definitive history but an initial, data-driven assessment of how airpower was used and what it accomplished under real-world constraints. (p. 6) They frame the campaign around the U.S. objective of ISIS‚Äôs ‚Äúenduring defeat,‚Äù emphasizing that this meant dismantling its territorial caliphate and proto-state institutions, not just attriting fighters. (p. 9) The chapter introduces their analytical focus on the close versus deep fight, arguing that the prioritization of the close fight reflected both strategic aims and partner needs. (p. 10, p. 35‚Äì36) It also previews the report‚Äôs structure, methods, and appendices.

  

**Key Points:**

- OIR is set against a backdrop of longstanding debates about airpower‚Äôs decisive potential, including criticism of the campaign as too limited. (p. 6, p. 12)
    
- The overriding objective was the **enduring defeat** of ISIS‚Äôs caliphate, making territorial control and governance institutions central targets. (p. 9)
    
- The authors explicitly differentiate between the **close fight** (inside the FSCL, mostly CAS) and the **deep fight** (strategic attack and interdiction beyond it) as their organizing framework. (p. 35‚Äì36)
    
- The study focuses on airpower‚Äôs contribution to a **partner-led, limited-liability** strategy, rather than hypothesizing about a U.S-led ground invasion that did not occur. (p. 9‚Äì10)
    
- They stress that political and legal constraints, as well as coalition dynamics, shaped the campaign design from the outset. (p. 6)
    
- The chapter positions OIR as part of a longer trend of air-centric, coalition campaigns in the post‚ÄìCold War era.
    

  

**Cross-Cutting Themes:**

- Airpower effectiveness is inseparable from **strategy and war aims**, not just sortie counts.
    
- Early **expectations vs reality**: promises of rapid coercion vs the realities of a grinding urban campaign.
    
- The importance of **coalition management** and legitimacy in contemporary air wars.
    
- OIR as a continuation of post-1991 **limited war** patterns: by/with/through partners, risk aversion, and tight ROE.
    

  

**Limits Map (mini):**

- **Political:** Domestic aversion to large-scale ground deployments; insistence on a limited-liability strategy. Source: exogenous (U.S. politics), largely fixed at strategic level.
    
- **Legal/Normative:** Strong LOAC and civilian-casualty concerns; coalition legitimacy requirements. Largely fixed but sometimes relaxable via refined weaponeering and better ISR.
    
- **Intelligence/Information:** Initial uncertainty about ISIS strengths and target systems; improved over time through data and partners. Adjustable via ISR investment.
    
- **Strategic:** Goal of ‚Äúenduring defeat‚Äù tied to territory, which inherently privileged the close fight over deep attack. (p. 9, p. 299)

#### **Section 1.1: Purpose of This Report**

**Summary:**

This section clarifies the report‚Äôs purpose: to provide a strategic and operational assessment of airpower in OIR that moves beyond polemics. It sets out key questions about how airpower was employed, its contributions to major ground battles, and how it shaped ISIS‚Äôs finances, logistics, and freedom of movement. **Key Points:**

- Evaluate airpower‚Äôs contribution to the **defeat of ISIS‚Äôs caliphate** and territorial rollback. (p. 9‚Äì10)
    
- Address whether political/ROE constraints materially limited the air campaign‚Äôs effectiveness. (p. 6)
    
- Inform future operational planning and doctrinal debates about air operations in limited wars.
    
- Clarify limits: not a comprehensive SOF or interagency history; focus is on airpower‚Äôs role.
    

  

**Cross-Cutting Themes:**

- **Evidence vs advocacy:** replacing anecdotal claims with data-driven analysis.
    
- Emphasis on **operational and strategic** levels, not sharp tactical adjudications.
    
- Awareness of **limits in knowledge** and the danger of overclaiming for or against airpower.
    

  

**Limits Map (mini):**

- **Resource/Time (analytic):** Finite ability to interview all participants; reliance on selected cases. (p. 8)

#### **Section 1.2: Methodology and Study Scope**

**Summary:**

The methodology section explains that the study combines **historiographical analysis**, two large custom data sets (CJTF-OIR strike releases and AFCENT airpower summaries), and more than 50 interviews with U.S. and coalition personnel. (p. 8, p. 393‚Äì394) The authors treat interviews as subjective perspectives to be triangulated with other sources, not as definitive accounts. (p. 8) They describe how they aggregated nearly 33,000 strikes and >81,000 targets, grouped them into meaningful categories (military forces, facilities/resources, terrain/LOCs, vehicles), and correlated this with campaign phases and major operations. (p. 398‚Äì400, p. 405) They also spell out the limitations in the data‚Äîsuch as changing definitions of ‚Äústrike,‚Äù the inclusion of artillery after 2017, and lack of explicit mapping between specific strikes and operations like Tidal Wave II‚Äîunderscoring that effectiveness judgments must be cautious. (p. 395‚Äì397, p. 401‚Äì403) The scope is confined to the Iraq/Syria theater from August 2014 to March 2019.

  

**Key Points:**

- Two core data sets: **RAND CJTF-OIR strike data** (by city, targets, effects) and **RAND AFCENT airpower summaries** (sorties, weapons, fuel, cargo). (p. 393‚Äì395)
    
- Targets grouped into four main bins: facilities/resources, military forces, terrain/LOCs, and vehicles. (p. 398‚Äì399)
    
- Data inconsistencies: evolving reporting practices, addition of artillery to ‚Äústrikes,‚Äù limited aircraft-type reporting after early 2017. (p. 395‚Äì397)
    
- Methodological rules to standardize ambiguous entries (e.g., ‚Äúmultiple‚Äù targets, multi-day releases, border cities). (p. 397‚Äì398)
    
- Use of interviews from USAF, Army, and coalition officers, including CFACCs and CJTF-OIR staff, cross-checked against documents and data. (p. 8)
    
- The assessment is **strategic/operational**, not tactical weapon-system performance.
    

  

**Cross-Cutting Themes:**

- The inherent **messiness of war data** and implications for measuring airpower effectiveness.
    
- Attention to **joint and coalition perspectives** in constructing the narrative.
    
- Methodological humility: ‚ÄúEven when there are enormous amounts of data and clearly defined operational goals, the criteria for military effectiveness are fuzzy and subject to interpretation.‚Äù (p. 8)
    

  

**Limits Map (mini):**

- **Information:** Non-uniform strike and sortie data; missing links between specific operations and strikes (e.g., Tidal Wave II). (p. 395‚Äì397, p. 399‚Äì400)
    
- **Resource/Time:** Limited interviews and non-exhaustive sampling of participants; adjustable only marginally. (p. 8)
    
- **Analytic/Conceptual:** ‚ÄúStrike‚Äù as a variable magnitude measure; imperfect mapping from tactical actions to strategic outcomes. (p. 396‚Äì397)

#### **Section 1.3: Airpower‚Äôs Contribution to OIR**

**Summary:**

This section advances the core claim that **airpower was ‚Äúindispensable to defeating ISIS‚Äù** yet operated within and in support of a broader political and partner-centric strategy. (p. 10, p. 298) The authors explain that Obama‚Äôs and then Trump‚Äôs approaches sought an **‚Äúenduring defeat‚Äù** at limited U.S. cost, prioritizing recapture of territory over punishment of ISIS leadership or coercive strategic bombing. (p. 9‚Äì10, p. 299) This made the close fight‚Äîsupporting Iraqi Security Forces (ISF), Kurdish Peshmerga, and the SDF‚Äîthe main locus of effort, with the deep fight (e.g., Tidal Wave II) playing a supporting role by constraining ISIS resources. (p. 35‚Äì36, p. 299) Airpower enabled local forces to conduct major offensives by delivering CAS, ISR, mobility, and precision fires, especially in urban fights like Mosul and Raqqa. It also provided critical **enablers**: ISR, tankers, DCA, and airlift allowed long-range, continuous strikes from Gulf bases and carriers. The authors stress that airpower alone could not clear and hold territory, but without it the partner strategy likely would have failed or required far greater ground casualties. (p. 10, p. 298‚Äì299)

  

**Key Points:**

- ‚ÄúAirpower was indispensable to defeating ISIS,‚Äù particularly as the **primary U.S. combat contribution** under a no-large-ground-forces policy. (p. 10)
    
- The **close fight** (CAS/counterland) received priority because ISIS‚Äôs center of gravity was its control of territory and population. (p. 35‚Äì36, p. 299)
    
- Deep operations targeted ISIS‚Äôs finances (Tidal Wave II, Point Blank), logistics, and GLOCs, but played a secondary role in achieving territorial rollback. (p. 204‚Äì205, p. 299)
    
- Airpower enabled coalition and partner ground forces while minimizing U.S. casualties, aligning with domestic political constraints. (p. 6, p. 9‚Äì10)
    
- Enabling functions‚ÄîISR, refueling, and airlift‚Äîwere critical and often more stressed than strike capacity. (p. 257‚Äì260, p. 273‚Äì285, p. 293)
    
- The overall verdict: OIR was successful and airpower was **critical** to that success, though not decisive by itself. (p. 298)
    

  

**Cross-Cutting Themes:**

- Airpower as **enabler of local partners**, not substitute for them.
    
- The **primacy of the close fight** when territorial control is the main strategic objective.
    
- Limits of strategic attack in counter-protostate wars.
    
- Alignment (and tension) between **political constraints** and operational design.
    

  

**Limits Map (mini):**

- **Strategic:** Limited-liability strategy; by/with/through partners; no U.S. large ground combat units‚Äîexogenous and fixed at political level. (p. 9‚Äì10)
    
- **Operational:** Dependence on partner ground tempo; basing distances from Gulf and carrier decks; urban terrain. Adjustable through basing improvements and increased enabling assets but not fully overcome. (p. 39‚Äì40, p. 273‚Äì288)
    
- **Legal/Normative:** Strict ROE and LOAC standards shaped munitions, target sets, and tolerance for collateral damage, especially in cities. Partially adjustable via better ISR and weaponeering.
    
- **Adversary Adaptation:** ISIS dispersed, embedded in civilians, and shifted to tunnels and VBIED tactics, reducing the deep fight‚Äôs leverage.

### **Chapter Two: The Air War Against the Islamic State, Phase I, August 2014‚ÄìMarch 2016**

  

**Summary:**

Chapter Two covers Phase I (‚Äúdegrade‚Äù), from the initial decision to intervene through March 2016. The authors show how ISIS‚Äôs rapid territorial gains in Iraq and Syria, especially the fall of Mosul and the Yazidi crisis on Sinjar, forced the U.S. to re-engage militarily, beginning with narrowly scoped air strikes to protect U.S. personnel and avert humanitarian catastrophe. (p. 15‚Äì17, p. 23‚Äì25) A broader multilateral strategy emerged in late 2014, formalizing OIR and creating CJTF-OIR, but political caution and the absence of capable local ground forces limited early offensive action. (p. 27‚Äì33, p. 49) Airpower began to ramp up as coalition partners joined, but drop rates were initially low due to stringent ROE, vetting requirements, and target scarcity. (p. 54, p. 78‚Äì82) By late 2015‚Äìearly 2016, improved partner capacity and institutional innovations like strike cells allowed more effective use of CAS and interdiction, contributing to key victories (e.g., Ramadi) and halting ISIS‚Äôs momentum. (p. 25‚Äì26, p. 63‚Äì65, p. 157‚Äì162) The chapter concludes that Phase I succeeded in stabilizing the front and setting conditions for later counteroffensives, but did not significantly roll back ISIS territory.

  

**Key Points:**

- Phase I begins with **reactive, narrowly scoped air operations** around Sinjar and Erbil, with humanitarian and force-protection objectives. (p. 23‚Äì25)
    
- A global coalition coalesces in late 2014, and OIR is formally named; CJTF-OIR is created under ARCENT leadership, with CFACC as supporting commander. (p. 27‚Äì33, p. 49‚Äì50)
    
- Early strike patterns emphasize **dynamic targeting and mobile ISIS forces**, with low weapons-release rates due to ROE and partner weaknesses. (p. 54, p. 63‚Äì65, p. 78‚Äì82)
    
- **Strike cells** emerge as a key adaptation for CAS when JTACs cannot accompany partners at the front. (p. 25‚Äì26)
    
- Phase I culminates in operations like **Kobani and Ramadi**, which demonstrate how improved partner forces plus heavier air support can turn the tide locally. (p. 142‚Äì147, p. 157‚Äì162)
    
- Overall effect: ISIS‚Äôs expansion is halted and degraded, but territorial rollback remains limited by partner capacity and political constraints.
    

  

**Cross-Cutting Themes:**

- **Crisis-driven intervention** under tight political limits.
    
- Early **mismatch** between American expectations of ‚Äúshock and awe‚Äù airpower and the realities of a dispersed, urbanized adversary.
    
- **Innovation under constraint** (strike cells, coalition C2).
    
- The importance of **time**: Phase I buys time to build partner forces at the cost of a prolonged campaign.
    

  

**Limits Map (mini):**

- **Political:** No large U.S. combat ground forces; narrow initial objectives focused on protection and humanitarian relief. Fixed, exogenous. (p. 15‚Äì17, p. 23‚Äì27)
    
- **Operational:** Limited basing and access early on; long ranges from Gulf bases; initially weak partner ground forces. Partly adjustable via coalition basing and train-and-equip programs. (p. 39‚Äì40)
    
- **Legal/Normative:** A high premium on civilian protection and host-nation consent constrained target sets and drop rates.
    
- **Intelligence:** Rapidly evolving situation and initially thin ISR picture slowed transition from reactive to offensive air employment.

#### **Section 2.1: The Decision to Intervene, June‚ÄìAugust 2014**

**Summary:**

This section traces how ISIS‚Äôs June 2014 offensive, culminating in the fall of Mosul and rapid advances toward Baghdad and Erbil, forced U.S. leaders to reconsider their post-2011 disengagement. (p. 15‚Äì17) The administration framed early air operations as limited actions to protect U.S. personnel and prevent genocide against the Yazidis trapped on Mount Sinjar. U.S. cargo aircraft, escorted by F-15s and F-16s, conducted airdrops‚Äîe.g., on August 7 a C-17 and two C-130s dropped 72 bundles of supplies to Yazidis‚Äîillustrating early blending of humanitarian and kinetic missions. (p. 23‚Äì25) The White House established ‚Äúred lines‚Äù for strikes (e.g., protecting Erbil and U.S. facilities, defending critical infrastructure such as Mosul Dam), reflecting a desire to avoid being drawn into Iraq‚Äôs internal politics. (p. 23‚Äì27) CENTCOM designated ARCENT as JFLCC on June 24, 2014, to coordinate security cooperation and later direct dynamic air strikes. (Appendix B, p. 349‚Äì350) The section shows that the initial design of OIR was **defensive and constrained**, not a pre-planned counteroffensive campaign.

  

**Key Points:**

- ISIS‚Äôs capture of Mosul and rapid advances toward Baghdad and Erbil are the strategic shock that triggers U.S. re-engagement. (p. 15‚Äì17)
    
- Initial U.S. objectives: protect Americans and facilities; avert genocide; defend **critical infrastructure** and major cities. (p. 23‚Äì27)
    
- Humanitarian airdrops on Mount Sinjar are enabled and protected by combat aircraft, foreshadowing air mobility‚Äôs central role. (p. 23‚Äì25)
    
- ARCENT becomes JFLCC and later morphs into CJTF-OIR, giving the Army the lead in early C2. (Appendix B, p. 349‚Äì350)
    
- Air operations initially focus on **dynamic CAS/interdiction** against ISIS forces threatening predefined red lines.
    
- The U.S. remains reluctant to re-enter Iraq‚Äôs politics, shaping narrow initial aims and rules.
    

  

**Cross-Cutting Themes:**

- **Limited war logic:** narrow objectives, careful escalation management.
    
- **Humanitarian triggers** for air intervention, reminiscent of Kosovo and Libya.
    
- Early **expectation that airpower can ‚Äúbuy time‚Äù** rather than immediately win.
    
- **Civil-military interplay**: presidential red lines bound CENTCOM‚Äôs options.
    

  

**Limits Map (mini):**

- **Political:** Strong domestic resistance to ‚Äúanother Iraq war‚Äù; explicit presidential lines about no ‚Äúcombat mission‚Äù for U.S. troops. Fixed, exogenous.
    
- **Legal/Normative:** Justification framed around R2P-like humanitarian grounds and defense of U.S. personnel; limits on operations inside Syria at first.
    
- **Operational:** Absence of U.S. advisers at the front, limited JTAC presence, forcing improvisations like remote strike cells. (p. 25‚Äì26)
    
- **Intelligence:** Limited visibility into ISIS‚Äôs deeper organization; focus on visible tactical formations.

#### **Section 2.2: A Multilateral Strategy to Defeat ISIS, September 2014**

**Summary:**

This section describes the pivot from ad hoc strikes to a more structured, coalition-based strategy. In September 2014, a broad international coalition forms, and the U.S. publicly articulates an objective to ‚Äúdegrade and ultimately destroy‚Äù ISIS, not just contain it. (p. 27‚Äì33) CENTCOM stands up CJTF-OIR and, on October 15, designates the campaign Operation Inherent Resolve, explicitly highlighting coalition resolve and a whole-of-government approach. (p. 49‚Äì50, p. 71) The coalition approach divides labor across lines of effort (military, foreign fighters, finance, counter-messaging), with air operations as the primary kinetic tool for the U.S. and many partners. The section notes that different coalition members adopted their own national operation names (Chammal, Shader, Okra, etc.), reflecting both unity and political caution. (Appendix B, p. 350) Yet the underlying U.S. strategy remains **limited liability and partner-centric**, with Iraqi forces expected to do most ground fighting, supported by airpower, advisors, and security assistance. (p. 9‚Äì10, p. 27‚Äì33)

  

**Key Points:**

- Public shift in rhetoric from narrow protection to **‚Äúdegrade and ultimately destroy‚Äù** ISIS. (p. 27‚Äì33)
    
- Formation of the **Global Coalition to Defeat ISIS**, with contributions of aircraft, trainers, and funding. (p. 33, fig. 2.2)
    
- Formal naming of OIR on October 15 and establishment of CJTF-OIR under LTG Terry; CFACC remains a separate, supporting commander. (p. 49‚Äì50, p. 71)
    
- Coalition partners use national operation names, highlighting domestic political needs for distance and control. (Appendix B, p. 350)
    
- Air operations expand into Syria, but host-nation consent issues and Syrian air defenses complicate planning. (p. 42‚Äì44)
    
- Strategy remains tied to **local ground partners**, not a U.S.-led invasion.
    

  

**Cross-Cutting Themes:**

- **Coalition politics** as both enabler and constraint on air employment.
    
- Strategic **ambiguity** between ‚Äúdestroy‚Äù vs ‚Äúdegrade‚Äù reflected in evolving targeting priorities.
    
- **Institutionalization** of the campaign: naming, CJTF, and formal command relationships.
    

  

**Limits Map (mini):**

- **Alliance/Political:** Need for broad coalition support and varying national caveats; Turkish sensitivities over Kurdish partners; Gulf states‚Äô priorities.
    
- **Legal:** Operations in Syria conducted without Assad regime consent, raising legality and escalation concerns; constraints on targeting regime assets.
    
- **Operational:** Multiple coalition air forces with different ROE and capabilities; airspace deconfliction challenges.
    
- **Strategic:** Lack of a clearly defined end state beyond ‚Äúdestroy ISIS,‚Äù complicating alignment of air efforts with political aims.

#### **Section 2.3: Going on the Offensive: Degrading ISIS, September 2014‚ÄìMarch 2016**

**Summary:**

Here the authors chart the gradual shift from reactive protection to more offensive air operations aimed at degrading ISIS forces, infrastructure, and leadership. Early operations in Kobani, Sinjar, and around Mosul demonstrate that when paired with determined local fighters, airpower can halt and reverse ISIS‚Äôs advances. #### **Section 4.2: Evolution in Coalition Processes and Capabilities**

**Summary:**

This section traces the evolution of air‚Äìground coordination processes, focusing on strike cells, TEA delegation, and integration of surface fires. (p. 156‚Äì157) These changes are presented as key learning points for future joint operations.

  

**Key Points:**

- **Tactical Directive 1** devolves TEA, allowing advisory commanders with terminal control capabilities to approve certain strikes, increasing responsiveness. (p. 133‚Äì134, p. 156)

**Cross-Cutting Themes:**

(p. 398‚Äì400, p. 405‚Äì408) They discuss inconsistencies such as changes in the definition of ‚Äústrike‚Äù (e.g., inclusion of artillery in 2017) and cumulative reporting in AFCENT summaries, as well as assumptions made to standardize data. (p. 395‚Äì397, p. 401‚Äì403) The appendix ends with snapshot figures summarizing strikes and targets by city and category, and weapons released over time. (p. 405‚Äì410, p. 427‚Äì428)

  

**Key Points:**

- CJTF-OIR strike releases provide daily strike and target data by city; AFCENT summaries provide monthly sortie and weapons data. (p. 393‚Äì395)
    
- Strike releases initially included only air-delivered strikes; later they included rocket artillery and eventually all ground-based tactical artillery. (p. 395‚Äì397)
    
- RAND grouped ~1,000 target descriptions into 773 types and then into four main bins. (p. 398‚Äì399)
    
- Additional bins for oil and gas targets (production; processing/refining; distribution/storage) support Tidal Wave II analysis. (p. 398‚Äì400)
    
- Assumptions were needed to handle multi-day releases, ambiguous entries, and recalculated AFCENT statistics. (p. 397‚Äì398, p. 401‚Äì403)
    
- Summary snapshots show that military-force targets were the most common (35% of total), and that strikes were distributed across many cities, with Mosul and Raqqa receiving the largest shares. (p. 405‚Äì408, p. 427‚Äì428)
    

  

**Cross-Cutting Themes:**

- The **difficulty of measuring airpower** with imperfect data.
    
- Necessity of clear **assumptions and transparency** in defense analysis.
    
- Importance of **target categorization** and MoE selection for interpreting strike data.
    

  

**Limits Map (mini):**

- **Information:** Data inconsistencies and evolving definitions; results must be interpreted cautiously. (p. 395‚Äì397, p. 401‚Äì403)
    
- **Analytic:** Aggregation and binning obscure some nuance but are necessary for tractability.
    
- **Methodological:** Cannot fully tie strikes to specific operations or distinguish primary vs incidental targets. (p. 396‚Äì397)
    
- **Resource:** Data collection and coding are labor-intensive and dependent on open sources.

## **üß± Limits Typology (case-specific)**

  

For OIR‚Äôs air war:

- **Political Limits:**
    
    - No large-scale U.S. ground combat units; reliance on **limited-liability, partner-centric** strategy. Fixed at the strategic level by domestic politics and presidential decisions. (p. 9‚Äì10)
        
    - Need to maintain a broad coalition, balance Turkish concerns over Kurdish partners, and avoid escalation with Russia/Iran constrained basing, targeting, and partner choices.
        
    - Adjustability: limited; some flexibility in adviser placement and national caveats, but basic structure fixed.
        
    
- **Legal/Normative Limits:**
    
    - Strong LOAC and civilian-protection standards constrained target selection and munitions, especially in urban battles (Mosul, Raqqa).
        
    - Requirement for Iraqi and Kurdish officers in strike cells to approve every bomb in Iraq added a host-nation legitimacy layer. (p. 25‚Äì26)
        
    - Largely fixed; partially mitigated by better ISR, weaponeering, and CDE tools.
        
    
- **Strategic Limits:**
    
    - Goal of ‚Äúenduring defeat‚Äù defined as **territorial destruction of the caliphate**, which privileged the close fight over deep strategic attack. (p. 9, p. 299)
        
    - Desire to avoid long-term occupation and nation-building restricted options to shape post-conflict governance.
        
    - Mostly fixed; could adjust emphasis (close vs deep) but not underlying ends.
        
    
- **Operational Limits:**
    
    - Basing distances from main Gulf sites and carriers increased reliance on tankers and limited responsiveness in some areas. (p. 39‚Äì40, p. 273‚Äì290)
        
    - Complex C2 with Army-led CJTF and supporting CFACC created seams in targeting and air‚Äìground integration. (Appendix B, p. 352‚Äì355)
        
    - Urban terrain and partner capability ceilings limited achievable tempo.
        
    - Adjustability: moderate via improved C2, TEA, strike cells, additional bases (Q-West, etc.), and better partner training.
        
    
- **Technological/Capability Limits:**
    
    - Finite stocks of PGMs, ISR platforms, and tankers; ability to surge limited by global force structure. (p. 273‚Äì290, p. 293)
        
    - Limited availability of scalable, low-collateral munitions for dense urban environments drove tough trade-offs.
        
    - Adjustability: partial via procurement, surge policies, and innovation, but constrained in the near term.
        
    
- **Intelligence/Information Limits:**
    
    - Weak early knowledge of ISIS networks and urban layouts; incomplete data on finances and logistics; imperfect strike-release and sortie data. (p. 8, p. 393‚Äì403)
        
    - Could not always distinguish primary vs incidental targets; uncertain MoE. (p. 396‚Äì397, p. 418)
        
    - Adjustability: improved through ISR, data fusion, and learning but never fully eliminated.
        
    
- **Adversary Adaptation Limits:**
    
    - ISIS dispersed forces, used tunnels, human shields, and urban sanctuaries, reducing the payoff from deep strikes and complicating CAS. (p. 63‚Äì65, p. 166‚Äì187, p. 180‚Äì187)
        
    - Adapted financially and logistically to Tidal Wave II/Point Blank and interdiction. (p. 108, p. 299)
        
    - Partly adjustable by changing tactics (e.g., VBIED-focused targeting, urban ISR), but structural.
        
    
- **Resource/Time Limits:**
    
    - Protracted campaign required sustained sortie, PGM, and tanker output; global demand for air assets (Afghanistan, Gulf air defense, Yemen) constrained OIR. (Appendix B, p. 352)
        
    - Political patience and coalition cohesion limited tolerance for extended, high-casualty operations.
        
    - Adjustability: some via improved efficiency and reprioritization, but not unlimited.
        
    

  

For each limit, adaptations included: strike cells and TEA devolution for ROE and adviser constraints; Tidal Wave II and GLOC interdiction for financial/logistics limitations; increased investment in ISR and PGMs for intelligence/legal needs; and refined C2 to mitigate Army‚Äìair seams.

## **üìè Measures of Effectiveness (MoE)**

- **What they tracked then:**
    
    - **Territory**: City and district control; the key MoE‚Äî‚ÄúBecause eliminating ISIS‚Äôs protostate was paramount, territory was the key measure of success in OIR.‚Äù (p. 299)
        
    - **Sorties & weapons released**: AFCENT summaries, often used as proxies for effort. (p. 294‚Äì295)
        
    - **Targets destroyed**: CJTF-OIR strike releases, grouped by target type (military forces, facilities/resources, terrain/LOCs, vehicles). (p. 398‚Äì399)
        
    - **ISIS revenue**: Estimated oil and non-oil revenue, used to assess effectiveness of Tidal Wave II and Point Blank. (p. 108, p. 204‚Äì230)
        
    - **Civilian casualties**: Monitored through internal processes and external reporting; a key political and normative indicator.
        
    - **Partner force performance**: Qualitative assessments of ISF, CTS, Peshmerga, and SDF effectiveness.
        
    
- **Better MoE today (with rationale):**
    
    - **Integrated territorial & governance metrics**: Not just km¬≤ but functioning local governance and security, to capture ‚Äúenduring‚Äù defeat.
        
    - **Civilian harm ratio**: Civilian casualties per ISIS fighter or per objective achieved, to link tactical choices to strategic legitimacy.
        
    - **Partner capacity indices**: Training levels, C2, and corruption metrics to gauge sustainability of gains.
        
    - **System disruption indices**: Degree of resilience in ISIS logistical and financial networks after deep operations, beyond simple counts of trucks or cash depots.
        
    - **Campaign efficiency metrics**: Effects (territory reclaimed, ISIS units destroyed) per unit of PGM, sortie, and tanker output, to assess resource use.
        
    - **Time-to-effect**: Lag between deep operations and observable impacts on ISIS operational behavior and territorial defense.
        
    
- **Evidence summary:**
    
    - RAND‚Äôs data show ~33,000 strikes and >81,000 targets engaged, distributed across ~30 main areas, with military forces as the largest target group (35%). (p. 405‚Äì408, p. 427‚Äì428)
        
    - Overall, Mosul and Raqqa received the largest share of strikes, aligning with their centrality as ISIS capitals. (p. 427‚Äì428)
        
    - Airpower activity peaked during Phase II and declined in Phase III, matching territorial trends. (p. 294‚Äì295)
        
    - Deep operations reduced ISIS‚Äôs oil revenue but did not independently determine territorial outcomes. (p. 108, p. 299)
        
    - The authors emphasize that even with robust data, **causal attribution remains difficult** and must be approached cautiously. (p. 8, p. 396‚Äì397, p. 418)

- **Role / position:** Pivotal local partners in northern Iraq (Mosul Dam, Sinjar) and northeastern Syria (Kobani, Raqqa).

- **Role / position:** Conduct leadership targeting, run SOF strike cells in Syria, and advise/assist SDF and other partners; manage high-end raids and CT operations. (Appendix B, p. 354‚Äì355)

### **ISIS Leadership and Fighters**

## **üß© Frameworks & Methods**

- **Levels of War:** The analysis is primarily **strategic and operational**, focusing on how air operations contributed to campaign-level objectives rather than tactical engagements or platform performance. (p. 8)
    
- **Mission Types:**
    
    - **Close fight:** CAS and counterland inside the FSCL in support of partner ground forces, particularly in urban battles. (p. 35‚Äì36, p. 129‚Äì195)
        
    - **Deep fight:** Deliberate strategic attack and interdiction beyond the FSCL, targeting finances (Tidal Wave II, Point Blank), logistics, and GLOCs. (p. 199‚Äì252)
        
    - **Enabling missions:** DCA, ISR, airlift, and aerial refueling. (p. 257‚Äì292)
        
    
- **Data-Driven Approach:**
    
    - CJTF-OIR strike releases -> RAND strike data set (strikes, targets by city, target types, effects). (p. 393‚Äì399)
        
    - AFCENT airpower summaries -> RAND airpower data set (sorties, weapons, fuel, cargo). (p. 395‚Äì401)
        
    - Grouping into target and system categories; linking to phases and operations. (p. 398‚Äì400, p. 405‚Äì408, p. 427‚Äì428)
        
    
- **Qualitative Methods:**
    
    - Historiographical method combining official documents, interviews, and data. (p. 8)
        
    - > 50 interviews with U.S. and allied personnel, plus AFHRA oral histories; cross-checking perceptions with data. (p. 8)
        
    
- **Conceptual Framework:**
    
    - Airpower is analyzed as part of a **by/with/through** strategy; emphasis on **territory as MoE** and close vs deep fight lens. (p. 9‚Äì10, p. 35‚Äì36, p. 299)
        
    - Recognition that **effectiveness criteria are fuzzy**, requiring mixed-method judgment. (p. 8)

## **üîÑ Learning Over Time (within the book & vs. prior SAASS 628 cases)**

- **What shifted?**
    
    - C2: From ad hoc ARCENT-led JFLCC to CJTF-OIR with more integrated strike cells and JACCE; TEA delegated down via Tactical Directive 1. (p. 49‚Äì50, p. 133‚Äì134, Appendix B, p. 350‚Äì355)
        
    - Air‚Äìground integration: Strike cells matured; surface fires integrated; partner forces became more capable, enabling more complex CAS. (p. 133‚Äì134, p. 156‚Äì157, p. 166‚Äì187, p. 180‚Äì187)
        
    - Deep operations: Shift from opportunistic strikes on high-value targets to sustained system campaigns against oil and finances. (p. 199‚Äì215, p. 226‚Äì231)
        
    - Enablers: Greater recognition of tankers and airlift as key constraints; DCA regained importance with Russian intervention. (p. 260‚Äì268, p. 273‚Äì290, p. 293)
        
    
- **What persisted?**
    
    - Limited-liability strategy with no large U.S. ground force; partner-centric approach from start to finish. (p. 9‚Äì10, p. 87‚Äì121)
        
    - Strong LOAC constraints and political focus on civilian casualties.
        
    - Debates over CAS vs strategic attack priorities and the relative importance of air vs ground components. (p. 6, p. 298‚Äì299)
        
    
- **What was (mis)learned?**
    
    - Some airpower advocates inferred that political constraints alone explain the campaign‚Äôs length, underestimating partner and terrain factors; the book pushes back on that mislearning. (p. 6, p. 298‚Äì299)
        
    - There is a risk of overlearning the lesson that deep strike is secondary, potentially underestimating its importance in other contexts (e.g., near-peer wars) where critical nodes may matter more.
        
    - Compared to prior cases like Desert Storm or Allied Force, OIR should reinforce that **context determines airpower roles**, but there is a tendency to project one campaign‚Äôs logic onto others.

## **üßê Critical Reflections**

- **Strengths:**
    
    - Deep, data-driven analysis with transparent methodology and careful treatment of uncertainties.
        
    - Balanced view that acknowledges both the indispensability and the limits of airpower in OIR. (p. 298‚Äì299)
        
    - Rich case studies of close and deep fights, providing concrete material for doctrinal and training discussions.
        
    - Clear articulation of how territory as MoE shaped the campaign and the relative roles of close vs deep operations. (p. 299)
        
    
- **Weaknesses / blind spots:**
    
    - Limited discussion of **civilian harm** and local political perceptions beyond acknowledging constraints; the political narrative in Iraq/Syria is relatively thin compared to the operational detail.
        
    - Less exploration of **intra-U.S. civil-military debates** over ROE, target approval, and strategy than some readers might desire.
        
    - May underplay the extent to which **ISIS ideology and social base** persisted despite territorial defeat, focusing more on military outcomes.
        
    - Because it is unclassified, some SOF and intelligence aspects are necessarily underdeveloped.
        
    
- **Unresolved questions:**
    
    - To what extent did deep operations accelerate ISIS‚Äôs defeat vs simply make fighting more expensive?
        
    - How sustainable is a partner-centric, limited-liability strategy in the face of long-term governance deficits?
        
    - How would OIR lessons translate to future campaigns against near-peer adversaries with robust air defenses?

## **‚öîÔ∏è Comparative Insights (link to prior course readings)**

- Compared to **Desert Storm**-type models (e.g., Wardenian strategic paralysis), OIR underscores that **urban, nonstate adversaries embedded in populations** are not susceptible to quick, decisive strategic bombing; the close fight and partner ground forces mattered far more.
    
- Relative to **Operation Allied Force** or **Libya 2011**, OIR demonstrates that airpower can support territorial conquest and holding (by partners) over multi-year campaigns, not just coercive bombing from afar, but at the cost of prolonged, resource-intensive operations and complex urban destruction.
    
- In line with **Afghanistan 2001/2002** (Lambeth‚Äôs work), OIR again validates the model of **airpower + small U.S. footprint + local partners**, but also shows that as adversaries adapt and fight in cities, the costs and time increase dramatically.
    
- Against more skeptical works (e.g., Pape‚Äôs _Bombing to Win_), the RAND study suggests that while airpower rarely achieves political victory alone, it can be **instrumental when integrated into a broader joint strategy and supported by capable partners**‚Äîa qualified but important pro-airpower finding.
    
- Across cases, OIR reinforces that **political constraints and MoE choices** (territory vs coercion vs punishment) heavily determine both what airpower is asked to do and how success is judged.

## **‚ùì Open Questions (for seminar)**

  

**1. What lessons did American strategists draw about air employment from their experiences prior to OIR?**

- From Desert Storm, Kosovo, Afghanistan, and Libya, strategists brought expectations that precision airpower could rapidly incapacitate adversaries, especially in permissive air environments, and that deep strike against critical nodes could generate disproportionate effects.
    
- The post-2001 experience also emphasized CAS in support of special operations and local partners, and reliance on ISR-driven targeting and small-footprint advisory missions.
    
- Strategists expected air superiority, robust coalition participation, and the ability to use deep operations (e.g., against leadership and infrastructure) as key tools in coercing or defeating adversaries.
    

  

**2. How accurate were those expectations in OIR?**

- Some expectations held: air superiority was easily achieved, coalition partners contributed meaningfully, and CAS in support of local forces proved highly effective. (p. 298‚Äì299)
    
- However, expectations of quick victory via deep strategic attack were **not** borne out; deep strikes against oil, cash, and logistics had limited independent impact on ISIS‚Äôs territorial control. (p. 299)
    
- Urban combat, human shields, and the proto-state‚Äôs reliance on territory and coercive governance rather than industrial infrastructure made OIR far more like prolonged urban attrition than ‚Äúshock-and-awe‚Äù coercion.
    
- Enabler demands (tankers, ISR) were higher and more constraining than earlier campaigns, challenging assumptions about resource sufficiency. (p. 273‚Äì290, p. 293)
    

  

**3. How did American strategists apply airpower in OIR?**

- They designed an air campaign to support a **by/with/through** strategy, prioritizing CAS and close counterland to enable ISF, Kurds, and SDF to retake territory. (p. 9‚Äì10, p. 35‚Äì36, p. 299)
    
- Airpower was applied in three main ways: close fight (CAS in battles like Mosul and Raqqa), deep fight (Tidal Wave II, Point Blank, logistics/GLOC interdiction), and enablers (DCA, ISR, mobility). (p. 129‚Äì195, p. 199‚Äì252, p. 257‚Äì292)
    
- Strategists used strike cells, TEA delegation, and integrated fires (air + artillery) to manage complex urban operations under tight ROE. (p. 133‚Äì134, p. 156‚Äì157)
    
- They employed systemic targeting of ISIS oil and finances to constrain resources, though as a shaping rather than decisive effort. (p. 204‚Äì230, p. 299)
    

  

**4. What limits did air employment face?**

- **Political limits:** No large U.S. ground force, heavy reliance on partners, coalition politics, and escalation concerns with Russia/Iran. (p. 6, p. 9‚Äì10, p. 260‚Äì268)
    
- **Legal/normative limits:** Strict LOAC and CDE constraints especially in urban environments; host-nation approval required for strikes in Iraq. (p. 25‚Äì26, p. 166‚Äì187, p. 180‚Äì187)
    
- **Operational limits:** Basing distances, partner capability ceilings, C2 complexity, and enabler shortages (tankers, ISR). (p. 39‚Äì40, p. 273‚Äì290, Appendix B, p. 350‚Äì352)
    
- **Adversary adaptation:** ISIS use of tunnels, human shields, VBIED belts, and dispersed networks reduced impact of both close and deep operations. (p. 63‚Äì65, p. 166‚Äì187, p. 180‚Äì187)
    
- **Information limits:** Imperfect intelligence, evolving definitions of ‚Äústrike,‚Äù and difficulty measuring effectiveness. (p. 8, p. 393‚Äì403)
    

  

**5. How does OIR ‚Äúfit‚Äù into the longer story of American airpower since 1945?**

- OIR extends the **post-1991 pattern** of air-centric, coalition, limited wars, where airpower is central but not independently decisive. Like Kosovo, Afghanistan, and Libya, it involves precision strikes, partner forces, and limited U.S. boots on the ground.
    
- It underscores a shift from large-scale industrial wars to **prolonged, urban, counter-protostate campaigns**, where CAS and enablers dominate and deep strategic attack plays a shaping role.
    
- It confirms that air superiority is often assumed but increasingly contested politically (Russia) and that enablers (especially tankers and ISR) are the critical bottlenecks. (p. 260‚Äì268, p. 273‚Äì290, p. 293)
    
- OIR strengthens the lesson that **airpower‚Äôs effectiveness is deeply contingent on strategy, partners, and MoE choice**, reinforcing themes from earlier limited-war cases while updating them for the urban, networked, nonstate context.
