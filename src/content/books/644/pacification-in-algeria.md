# **Pacification in Algeria, 1956‚Äì1958**

  

## **üéôÔ∏è Comps Prep (Oral Comprehensive Exam)**

- If insurgents can shelter inside population control (cells + terror), then tactical attrition will not end the war, because ‚Äúthe objective is the population‚Äù and the fight is for control/support. So what for strategy: reframe success as _organized_ civilian alignment, not enemy KIA. (p. 246)
    
- If the counterinsurgent expects spontaneous ‚Äúhearts and minds,‚Äù then pacification stalls, because support is not spontaneous and must be organized through an identifiable pro-government minority that can pull neutrals over time. So what for strategy: build and protect a local political base before chasing ‚Äúdevelopment‚Äù effects. (pp. 246‚Äì247)
    
- If the counterinsurgent cannot saturate the whole battlespace, then trying to do everything everywhere fails, because control/support tasks force an ‚Äúarea by area‚Äù approach and demand early visible wins to change civilian expectations about who will prevail. So what for strategy: sequence the campaign and prioritize early proof of victory. (pp. 246‚Äì247)
    
- If IW is ‚Äústrategic‚Äù in the political sense, then Galula‚Äôs micro-level pacification logic aligns with Kalyvas‚Äôs control‚Üícollaboration intuition: civilians shift behavior once a side demonstrates durable local control and protection. Bridge: this aligns with Kalyvas on collaboration/control and usefully complements Simpson‚Äôs ‚Äúwar as politics‚Äù frame. (p. 209; pp. 246‚Äì247)
    

  

## **Online Description**

  

A first-person RAND memorandum reconstructing David Galula‚Äôs 1956‚Äì1958 experience in Kabylia during the Algerian War, focused on ‚Äúpacification‚Äù as the central operational problem: controlling and winning population support rather than relying on military operations alone. ## **60‚ÄëSecond Brief**

- **Core claim (1‚Äì2 sentences):** In insurgency/counterinsurgency, the decisive struggle is political control of the population; destroying guerrillas and holding terrain are insufficient if the counterinsurgent cannot control and organize civilian support. (pp. 246‚Äì247; Summary, pp. xix‚Äìxxi)
    
- **Causal logic in a phrase:** _Control ‚Üí protected minority ‚Üí organized support ‚Üí intelligence + isolation of insurgents ‚Üí durable pacification._ (pp. 246‚Äì247; Summary, pp. xix‚Äìxxii)
    
- **Why it matters for IW / strategic competition (2‚Äì4 bullets):**
    
    - Population behavior follows perceived victor/protector more than ideology over time, so signaling resolve/consistency is an operational variable. (p. 247; Summary, pp. xxii‚Äìxxiii)
        
    - Pacification requires coercion + governance sequencing; ‚Äúarea by area‚Äù is not a preference but a constraint of manpower and control tasks. (p. 247)
        
    - Local success can be real yet strategically fragile if national politics are inconsistent; cabinet crises and policy uncertainty degrade local alignment. (Summary, pp. xxii‚Äìxxiii)
        
    
- **Best single takeaway (1 sentence):** Measure progress by _organized_ civilian cooperation under durable protection, not by tactical metrics. (pp. 246‚Äì247)
    

  

## **Course Lens**

- **How does this text define/illuminate irregular warfare?**
    
    - IW is a contest for political control where the population is ‚Äúthe real terrain of the war,‚Äù and violence/operations are means to affect that political balance. (p. 246; p. 209)
        
    - Insurgent momentum can start with ‚Äúblind terrorism‚Äù for publicity/fear, then ‚Äúselective terrorism‚Äù to lock in local control. (Summary, p. xvii)
        
    
- **What does it imply about power/control, success metrics, and timeline in IW?**
    
    - **Power/control:** Durable control is demonstrated through protection + policing + organizational penetration of villages (census, movement control, dismantling cells). (Summary, pp. xx‚Äìxxi)
        
    - **Success metrics:** Whether civilians cooperate openly, provide intelligence, and participate in local governance/defense structures‚Äîi.e., organized support. (pp. 246‚Äì247; Summary, pp. xix‚Äìxxii)
        
    - **Timeline:** Counterinsurgency seldom ends cleanly; final phases can persist for years even after major successes. (p. 245)
        
    
- **How does it connect to strategic competition?**
    
    - Political credibility/consistency is decisive: local gains can be undercut by national-level vacillation that signals possible abandonment. (Summary, pp. xxii‚Äìxxiii)
        
    - Colonial/global context shapes legitimacy and ‚Äúpsychological superiority,‚Äù constraining counterinsurgent strategy even with material dominance. (p. 245; Summary, p. xvii)

## **‚úÖ Direct Responses to Seminar Questions**

- **Q:** What was the political relationship between France and Algeria? How did global context shape French COIN?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - Galula frames Algeria as a colonial conflict: ‚Äúa colonial war on our part and a war for independence on the part of the FLN,‚Äù with ‚Äúthe ‚Äòtide of history‚Äô‚Äù favoring the insurgents‚Äô cause psychologically. (p. 245)
            
        - Demography/power relations mattered: nine million Muslims lived alongside one million Europeans who dominated political/economic life, shaping both grievances and the optics of rule. (Summary, p. xvii)
            
        - International environment was permissive for the FLN: Arab and communist countries favored rebellion; global opinion was not unsympathetic to nationalist anti-colonial aims. (Summary, p. xvii)
            
        - French political weakness/instability (Metropolitan cabinet churn) fed doubts among Algerians about the credibility of French promises and the durability of local alignment. (p. 243; Summary, pp. xxii‚Äìxxiii)
            
        
    
- **Q:** Main objectives of French approach?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - Strategic objective recognized (at least in consensus): ‚Äúdivorc[ing] the rebels from the population,‚Äù since ‚Äúmilitary operations could not by themselves bring a complete, definitive victory.‚Äù (pp. 64‚Äì65)
            
        - Operational objective in Galula‚Äôs formulation: control and then win/support the population‚Äî‚Äúthe objective is the population.‚Äù (p. 246)
            
        - Build organized support by identifying a pro-government minority and using it to influence neutrals, rather than assuming sympathy equals support. (Summary, pp. xix‚Äìxx)
            
        - Practical pacification instruments included village-level control measures (census, movement checks), dismantling political cells, and then building local governance/auxiliary forces and civic programs. (Summary, pp. xx‚Äìxxii)
            
        - Late-war institutionalization: only in 1959 did theater command issue a comprehensive pacification blueprint (Plan Challe) incorporating methods proven in practice. (Summary, p. xxiii)
            
        
    
- **Q:** How effective were French methods?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - At the local level, Galula presents a case of strong effectiveness: purge methods dismantled village cells, and safeguards plus dispersal of small detachments helped prevent re-infiltration; rebels eventually abandoned the Aissa Mimoun range ‚Äúas a lost cause.‚Äù (Summary, p. xxi)
            
        - Behavioral indicator: once French control was visible, villagers shifted from avoidance to open friendliness and willingness to defy insurgent rules‚Äîsuggesting control changed the collaboration calculus. (Summary, pp. xxi‚Äìxxii)
            
        - Scaling problem: before Plan Challe, Algeria remained a ‚Äúcheckerboard‚Äù of pacification quality depending on individual commander initiative/conviction. (Summary, p. xxiii)
            
        - Strategic fragility: cabinet crises and policy uncertainty had adverse psychological effects on cooperative villages and emboldened insurgents, limiting what local effort could achieve. (Summary, pp. xxii‚Äìxxiii)
            
        - War-level paradox in Galula‚Äôs account: military success could be decisive tactically, yet political factors could determine the ultimate outcome. (Introduction, pp. 1‚Äì2; Preface, p. xi)
            
        
    
- **Q:** How well does Kalyvas explain French/Algerian interactions?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - Strong fit on **control‚Üícollaboration**: Galula describes a ‚Äúbarrier of silence‚Äù driven by insurgent surveillance/cells and fear of reprisals; cooperation increases after cells are destroyed and control is demonstrable. (Summary, pp. xx‚Äìxxi)
            
        - Strong fit on **selective/targeted mechanisms**: purge methods rely on identifying cell members and isolating militant supporters within villages, not indiscriminate force‚Äîconsistent with micro-logic where information and local control shape violence/collaboration. (Summary, pp. xx‚Äìxxi)
            
        - Strong fit on **civilian risk calculus**: villagers cooperate when they believe French protection will persist; when national politics wobble, cooperation becomes dangerous and declines. (Summary, pp. xxii‚Äìxxiii)
            
        - Potential tension: Galula‚Äôs account emphasizes campaign design and administrative control tools (census/movement systems) as levers; Kalyvas‚Äôs framework may treat these as downstream of control rather than central instruments (inference).
            
        - Bottom line: Galula supplies granular mechanisms (cells, policing, village governance) that operationalize how ‚Äúcontrol‚Äù is produced, not just observed (inference).
            
        
    
- **Q:** How did this experience influence American COIN theory/ops?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - The book itself documents transmission pathways into U.S. professional/academic circuits: Galula attended the U.S. Armed Forces Staff College and later served at Harvard‚Äôs Center for International Affairs. (Preface, pp. ix‚Äìx)
            
        - RAND/ARPA context: the memorandum was produced within RAND‚Äôs counterinsurgency research for ARPA‚Äôs Project AGILE, situating it in U.S. defense analytic ecosystems. (Preface, p. xi)
            
        - The 2006 foreword explicitly frames continuing relevance for U.S. challenges in Iraq and ‚Äúinsurgencies elsewhere,‚Äù implying its use as a learning source in modern U.S. COIN debates. (Foreword to New Edition, p. vii)
            
        - Substantively, Galula‚Äôs codified ‚Äúlaws‚Äù and practical pacification technique offer a portable doctrine-like set of principles that U.S. COIN practitioners could adopt/adapt (e.g., population as objective; area-by-area sequencing). (pp. 246‚Äì247; Appendix 3, p. 298)
            
        - Any claim about direct doctrinal adoption by specific U.S. manuals/units is **TBD** without external sourcing (and is not established by this PDF).
