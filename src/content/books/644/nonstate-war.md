# **Nonstate Warfare**

  

_The Military Methods of Guerillas, Warlords, and Militias_

  

## **üéôÔ∏è Comps Prep (Oral Comprehensive Exam)**

- If you assume ‚Äúnonstate = guerilla,‚Äù then you will misread enemy behavior and misbuild forces, because state and nonstate methods overlap along a Fabian‚ÄìNapoleonic spectrum. So what for strategy: plan for midspectrum nonstate opponents, not categories. (PDF pp.29‚Äì30)
    
- When a nonstate actor faces a materially lethal battlefield and usable inferiority, then more Fabian methods become optimal, because war making balances the firepower needed to control territory against the cover needed to survive. So what for strategy: estimate that tradeoff before predicting ‚Äúirregular.‚Äù (PDF p.45)
    
- When institutions are weak and stakes are limited, then nonstate actors tend to default toward simpler, often more Fabian behavior (or execute midspectrum poorly), because midspectrum warfare demands training and specialist interdependence their politics can‚Äôt reliably sustain. So what for strategy: treat organization/internal governance as a decisive variable in IW. (PDF p.64)
    
- This book supports Kalyvas‚Äôs focus on control/collaboration by reframing ‚Äúhow actors fight‚Äù as a product of organizational capacity and stakes, and it complicates Maoist assumptions of inevitable progression by making movement along the spectrum contingent on politics and technology. (PDF p.64)
    

  

## **Online Description**

  

Biddle examines how nonstate armed groups actually fight‚Äîand argues that treating ‚Äúconventional‚Äù state war and ‚Äúguerilla‚Äù nonstate war as distinct categories is misleading. The book explains variation in nonstate military methods using a deductive theory and historical case studies that emphasize material constraints and (especially) internal politics such as institutions and stakes. (PDF pp.11, 45, 64)

## **60‚ÄëSecond Brief**

- Core claim (1‚Äì2 sentences): Nonstate actors‚Äô military methods vary along a spectrum from Fabian (irregular) to Napoleonic (conventional), and this variation is best explained by the interaction of material incentives and internal political constraints (institutions and stakes), not by stereotypes about ‚Äúguerillas,‚Äù materiel alone, or ‚Äútribal culture.‚Äù (PDF p.18; PDF p.64)
    
- Causal logic in a phrase: **Firepower‚Äìcover tradeoff** (numbers/technology) √ó **political feasibility** (institutions/stakes) ‚Üí **where an actor can fight on the Fabian‚ÄìNapoleonic spectrum**. (PDF pp.45, 64)
    
- Why it matters for IW / strategic competition (2‚Äì4 bullets):
    
    - Nonstate actors can be _midspectrum_ and ‚Äústate-like,‚Äù producing serious tactical/operational challenges (e.g., Hezbollah). (PDF pp.107‚Äì108)
        
    - Partner capacity and adversary threat are often constrained less by weapons than by internal politics and ability to coordinate specialists. (PDF pp.64, 138)
        
    - Technology diffusion widens the range of plausible enemy methods; US force design should avoid optimizing for one narrow type of war. (PDF p.223)
        
    
- Best single takeaway (1 sentence): **Stop assuming ‚Äúnonstate = irregular‚Äù‚Äîdiagnose institutions, stakes, and technology to predict how an armed group will actually fight.** (PDF pp.21, 64)
    

  

## **Course Lens**

- How does this text define/illuminate **irregular warfare**?
    
    - Treats ‚Äúirregular/guerilla‚Äù as the **Fabian extremum** on a **continuum** of military methods, not a distinct ‚Äútype of war.‚Äù (PDF p.22; PDF p.29)
        
    - Fabian characteristics (as framed here): refusal to hold ground via decisive engagement, dispersion, civilian intermingling for concealment, reliance on coercion, and light weapons suited to concealment. (PDF p.22)
        
    
- What does it imply about **power/control**, **success metrics**, and **timeline** in IW?
    
    - Control is central: even ‚Äúwars of profit‚Äù still require shaping collective choices of populations over power/resources. (PDF p.31)
        
    - ‚ÄúSuccess‚Äù is not just attrition; it‚Äôs achieving political effects via military methods that can credibly **coerce, protect, or control** populations/territory‚Äîsometimes requiring holding ground (midspectrum/Napoleonic tendencies). (PDF pp.45, 106)
        
    - Timeline depends on feasible methods: actors constrained to Fabian approaches may accept protraction; actors capable of midspectrum methods may seek faster territorial effects‚Äîbut feasibility is political as well as material. (PDF pp.64, 81)
        
    
- How does it connect to **strategic competition**?
    
    - Predicts a wide future distribution of enemy methods as advanced weapons proliferate and internal politics continue to vary‚Äîraising the premium on adaptable forces and accurate threat diagnosis. (PDF p.223)

## **‚úÖ Direct Responses to Seminar Questions**

- **Q:** What does Biddle define nonstate warfare? Key characteristics vs traditional state-centric warfare?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - **Scope:** explains the **military behavior of nonstate actors in warfare involving numerically superior state opponents since 1900**. (PDF p.21)
            
        - **Nonstate actor (definition):** any entity other than a sovereign state as defined by the Montevideo Convention (includes insurgents, separatists, warlord militias, PMFs, extremists, criminal syndicates, etc.). (PDF p.21)
            
        - **Warfare (definition):** organized violence exceeding **1,000 total battle deaths**, with at least **100 deaths on each of at least two sides**. (PDF p.21)
            
        - **Key move vs ‚Äútraditional‚Äù state-centric warfare:** ‚Äústate‚Äù vs ‚Äúnonstate‚Äù is an **actor identity**, not a reliable predictor of **methods**; methods are better understood along a **Fabian‚ÄìNapoleonic spectrum** with substantial overlap. (PDF p.29; PDF p.22)
            
        - **Operational distinction:** irregular (Fabian) vs conventional (Napoleonic) is defined behaviorally (holding ground, dispersion, distinguishability, etc.), not by legal sovereignty. (PDF p.22)
            
        
    
- **Q:** What assumptions about wartime behavior is he countering? Does it need to be made?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - Counters the assumption that state and nonstate war making are **mutually exclusive categories** (‚Äúconventional‚Äù vs ‚Äúguerilla/irregular‚Äù), even when a ‚Äúhybrid‚Äù category is added. (PDF p.29)
            
        - Counters the idea that the ‚Äúguerilla‚Äù model is the natural/necessary method for nonstate actors; he calls this intuition a **fallacy** because many nonstate actors do not conform. (PDF pp.29‚Äì30)
            
        - Counters ‚Äútribal culture‚Äù as a sufficient explanation of nonstate methods: ‚Äúneither materiel nor tribal culture offers an adequate explanation‚Äù for observed patterns. (PDF p.18)
            
        - Does the assumption need to be made? The book argues **no**‚Äîbecause it obscures the real causal drivers (material incentives + internal politics), and can mislead policy/force design. (PDF pp.11, 64)
            
        
    
- **Q:** What drives nonstate actors toward irregular warfare?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - **Material logic:** When the battlefield and relative capabilities make survival difficult, more Fabian approaches become attractive because actors must trade firepower for cover/concealment. (PDF p.45)
            
        - **Political feasibility logic:** Even when midspectrum behavior is militarily advantageous, actors may default to simpler (often more Fabian) methods if institutions and stakes don‚Äôt support the training, coordination, and specialist interdependence required. (PDF p.64)
            
        - **Stakes mechanism:** leaders invest and accept sacrifice for military capability when outcomes are perceived as more important (higher ‚Äústakes‚Äù). (PDF p.81)
            
        - **Institutions mechanism:** institutional capacity affects the ‚Äúspan of trust,‚Äù monitoring/enforcement, and ability to solve collective action dilemmas that complex warfare creates. (PDF pp.64, 78)
            
        - Empirical illustration: Hezbollah could exploit midspectrum advantages; the JAM could not due to internal politics. (PDF p.138)
            
        
    
- **Q:** How do technology/materiel affect whether actors fight irregularly?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - Technology shapes the **materially optimal** point on the spectrum by altering the tradeoff between firepower (needed to control territory) and cover (needed to survive). (PDF p.45)
            
        - Over time, technology change shifts incentives: early 20th-century optimal nonstate behavior was ‚Äúhighly Fabian,‚Äù but technology has moved the optimum ‚Äútoward the middle‚Äù for more than a generation. (PDF p.45)
            
        - Nonstate actors can field sophisticated systems, sometimes approaching state enemies (book provides examples of advanced weapons held/used by nonstate forces). (PDF p.54)
            
        - Technology is not determinative: the SNA had TOW missiles but made no recorded use of them, indicating organizational/political constraints can override mat√©riel opportunity. (PDF p.166)
            
        - Period effects: Vietcong 1965‚Äì68 were especially Fabian (0.2 on the six-point scale), consistent with a different technological/systemic environment than 2000s-era conflicts. (PDF p.213)
            
        
    
- **Q:** Does Biddle help us understand state actors and IW?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - Yes, by arguing for the **possibility of a unified theory of state and nonstate war making** grounded in shared material realities and variation in institutions and stakes. (PDF p.226)
            
        - His framework makes ‚Äúirregular warfare‚Äù intelligible as one end of a broader continuum of methods‚Äîuseful for states as well as nonstates, especially where states employ proxies or adopt more Fabian tactics themselves. (PDF p.29; PDF p.226)
            
        - He explicitly notes that states vary in institutions and stakes too‚Äîraising the question of whether similar relationships hold for state methods. (PDF p.226)
            
        
    
- **Q:** Are his policy recommendations convincing?
    
    - **A:**
        
        - His central policy warning is convincing on its own terms: future enemy methods may span the spectrum, so **optimizing narrowly** against one subset raises risk of mismatch. (PDF p.223)
            
        - He argues a future ‚Äúmodal‚Äù opponent implies a force that looks more like the **2001 legacy force** than extreme alternatives‚Äîan argument grounded in anticipated enemy variation and complex terrain requirements. (PDF p.224)
            
        - Limits (as inference): the persuasiveness depends on whether his assumed drivers (proliferation + persistent political variation) remain dominant relative to other strategic shifts (e.g., maritime/air-centric contests outside his scope). (PDF pp.21, 223)

## **Theory / Framework Map**

- Level(s) of analysis:
    
    - Primarily **organizational** (nonstate actor internal politics) interacting with **systemic/material** battlefield conditions. (PDF pp.45, 64)
        
    
- Unit(s) of analysis:
    
    - Nonstate actor behavior in warfare involving numerically superior states (case-based, operationalized in appendix). (PDF p.21; PDF p.229)
        
    
- Dependent variable(s):
    
    - **Military behavior** on the **Fabian‚ÄìNapoleonic spectrum** (including midspectrum variation). (PDF p.22; PDF p.45)
        
    
- Key independent variable(s):
    
    - Material: **numerical imbalance**, **technological sophistication**. (PDF p.45)
        
    - Politics: **institutionalization**, **stakes**. (PDF p.45; PDF p.81)
        
    
- Mechanism(s):
    
    - Material conditions define a **militarily optimal** compromise between firepower (control) and cover (survival). (PDF p.45)
        
    - Institutions and stakes determine whether complex midspectrum behavior is **politically achievable**, via training capacity, specialist interdependence, and collective action solutions. (PDF p.64; PDF p.78)
        
    
- Scope conditions / where it should NOT apply:
    
    - Applies to warfare (as defined) since 1900; excludes war at sea and WMD use; not dispositive for all purely inter-nonstate warfare. (PDF p.21; PDF p.229)
        
    
- Observable implications / predictions:
    
    - Over time, technology shifts material incentives toward midspectrum, but internal political variation ensures wide dispersion of observed methods. (PDF pp.45, 223)
        
    - Mature institutions + high stakes ‚Üí greater ability to implement complex midspectrum methods; weak institutions/limited stakes ‚Üí simpler (often more Fabian) behavior or poor execution. (PDF pp.64, 81, 138)
        
    

  

## **Key Concepts & Definitions (author‚Äôs usage)**

- **Nonstate actor**
    
    - Definition: any entity other than a Montevideo Convention sovereign state. (PDF p.21)
        
    - Role in argument: sets scope of ‚Äúnonstate warfare‚Äù and separates actor identity from method.
        
    - Analytical note: broad category‚Äîrequires additional variables (institutions/stakes/tech) to predict behavior.
        
    
- **Warfare**
    
    - Definition: organized violence exceeding 1,000 battle deaths with at least 100 on each of two sides. (PDF p.21)
        
    - Role in argument: excludes low-scale violence and focuses on national-security-relevant wars.
        
    - Analytical note: scope boundary matters‚Äîmany ‚Äúterrorism‚Äù cases qualify if violence threshold met. (PDF p.21)
        
    
- **Fabian methods**
    
    - Definition (archetype): unwillingness to defend ground via decisive engagement; dispersion; concealment via civilian intermingling; coercion; preference for light weapons. (PDF p.22)
        
    - Role: one pole of dependent-variable spectrum (irregular extreme).
        
    - Analytical note: ‚ÄúFabian‚Äù ‚â† always low skill; can still demand complex tactical coordination (e.g., VC ambush). (PDF p.211)
        
    
- **Napoleonic methods**
    
    - Definition (archetype): insistence on decisive engagement to seize/hold ground; local concentration; uniformed forces separated from population centers; brute force; preference for heavy weapons. (PDF p.22)
        
    - Role: other pole of spectrum (conventional extreme).
        
    - Analytical note: even nonstate actors can attempt (and sometimes approximate) Napoleonic behavior. (PDF p.192)
        
    
- **Midspectrum**
    
    - Definition: behavior between pure Fabian and pure Napoleonic; most real war is ‚Äúinterpenetrated‚Äù compromise between lethality and survivability. (PDF p.49)
        
    - Role: explains why ‚Äúhybrid‚Äù is better treated as continuous variation.
        
    - Analytical note: midspectrum behavior is often hardest to execute, creating selection on institutions/stakes. (PDF p.64)
        
    
- **Materially optimal behavior**
    
    - Definition: the militarily optimal choice of methods given numerical imbalance and technological sophistication. (PDF p.45)
        
    - Role: baseline prediction before politics constraints.
        
    - Analytical note: emphasizes **usable** advantages, not just raw inventory.
        
    
- **Politically achievable behavior**
    
    - Definition: feasible behavior given internal political constraints that can prevent implementing the militarily optimal method. (PDF p.45; PDF p.64)
        
    - Role: explains why similar mat√©riel can yield different methods (Hezbollah vs JAM). (PDF p.138)
        
    - Analytical note: useful for diagnosing partner limitations and adversary weaknesses.
        
    
- **Institutions (institutionalization / ‚Äúnatural order‚Äù taxonomy)**
    
    - Definition: formal/informal mechanisms for rules, enforcement, monitoring, and managing violence; includes ‚Äúinformal natural order‚Äù category common among nonstates. (PDF p.78)
        
    - Role: shapes span of trust and capacity for coordinated specialization.
        
    - Analytical note: operationalizable via observable governance and command/control features (appendix). (PDF p.229)
        
    
- **Stakes**
    
    - Definition: ‚Äúabsolute value of the perceived expected utility‚Ä¶ of the war‚Äôs potential outcomes‚Äù for senior leadership. (PDF p.81)
        
    - Role: links perceived importance to willingness to invest/sacrifice for capability.
        
    - Analytical note: can shift over time (e.g., targeted capture making stakes existential). (PDF p.165)
        
    
- **Dispersion / force-to-space ratio**
    
    - Definition: modern lethality and need for cover systematically drive dispersion and expansion of battlespace. (PDF p.58)
        
    - Role: helps explain why complex coordination is increasingly demanding.
        
    - Analytical note: dispersion raises command/control and logistics burdens‚Äîkey for comparing actors‚Äô institutional capacity.

## **‚öñÔ∏è Assumptions & Critical Tensions**

- Assumptions the author needs:
    
    - A rationalist logic: in ‚Äústeady state,‚Äù most combatants will approximate militarily optimal behavior; those who don‚Äôt ‚Äúshould suffer‚Äù for failure. (PDF p.24)
        
    - The spectrum operationalization captures meaningful variance in methods (appendix coding). (PDF p.229)
        
    
- Tensions / tradeoffs / contradictions:

- What would change the author‚Äôs mind? (inference)
    
    - Robust cases where institutions/stakes are weak/limited yet actors reliably implement complex midspectrum warfare _without_ strong external scaffolding, or where mature institutions/high stakes still systematically produce extreme Fabianism in modern tech settings.

## **Policy & Strategy Takeaways**

- Implications for the US + partners:
    
    - Expect a **wide distribution** of enemy methods (from very irregular to very state-like) as advanced weapons spread and internal politics varies. (PDF p.223)
        
    - Force design should avoid narrow optimization; ‚Äúmodal‚Äù future opposition implies capabilities closer to the 2001 legacy force than extreme ‚Äúalternatives.‚Äù (PDF p.224)
        
    
- Practical ‚Äúdo this / avoid that‚Äù bullets:
    
    - Do: build analytic diagnostics for **institutions + stakes + tech** before predicting tactics or advising partners. (PDF pp.64, 81)
        
    - Do: assume some nonstate actors can execute coherent theater designs and disciplined defense in complex terrain. (PDF p.108)
        
    - Avoid: equating weapons possession with operational competence; inventory can be unused or misused for political reasons. (PDF p.166)
        
    - Avoid: building doctrine solely around ‚ÄúCOIN light infantry‚Äù or ‚Äústandoff precision‚Äù as universal solutions. (PDF pp.223‚Äì225)
        
    
- Risks / second-order effects:
    
    - Targeting leadership can shift stakes and induce behavioral change (potentially more territorial fighting), with escalation and civilian harm risks. (PDF p.165)
        
    - Training/advising can fail if partner institutions cannot absorb specialist interdependence‚Äîrisking wasted effort or factional capture. (PDF pp.64, 192)
        
    
- What to measure (MOE/MOP ideas) and over what timeline:
    
    - Institutional indicators: command/control robustness, internal enforcement/discipline capacity, factionalism and ‚Äúspan of trust.‚Äù (PDF pp.64, 78)
        
    - Stakes indicators: leadership threat perceptions, willingness to accept casualties, mobilization and investment behaviors. (PDF p.81)
        
    - Method indicators: willingness to hold ground, distinguishability/uniforming, reliance on coercion vs brute force, functional differentiation in theater. (PDF p.22; PDF p.229)
